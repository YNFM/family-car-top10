How to choose

These are my reads on each car, not a ranking — the ranking is above, and it's yours. A car greyed out here isn't in your current filter.

Maximum space + safety → the Kia Carnival or Hyundai Staria: genuine 8–9 seaters, both with 5★ ratings on current protocols (2021 and 2022). The Carnival is the more resolved all-rounder at 5,155 mm; the Staria is longer still at 5,253 mm and seats up to nine.
Maximum safety, low theft risk, tighter budget → the used Kia Sorento or Hyundai Santa Fe: 5★, full driver-assist and leather for ~R475–500k. Tighter still, the used Honda BR-V at ~R330k — a 5★ with Honda Sensing and strong reliability and resale.
Buy new with a full warranty → the Mahindra XUV700 AX7L (Global NCAP 5★ adult, 7 airbags, ~R600k) or the flagship Kia Carens SX — eyes open on its 3★ adult crash score.
Premium badge → the Mercedes-Benz GLB: a genuine Mercedes cabin and a 5★ for ~R620k used (scoped to a verified 7-seater), but a tight 5+2 third row and out-of-warranty running costs well above the Korean and Japanese cars here.
Toughness & resale, if you accept the theft risk → the Toyota Fortuner, and at R650k that's the plusher 2.8 VX. It is the one car in this set with a genuine, multi-source hijacking case against it — SA's published lists put it as high as #4, and it shares its parts with the #1-targeted Hilux — which is why it carries the penalty. Set the penalty to zero in the panel above and you'll see where it lands without it. Only worth it with a tracker, gear-lock, secure parking and the higher insurance premium such cars attract.

My own pick on the criteria I was given: the used Kia Carnival — an 8-seat cabin, a 2021-protocol 5★, and a low theft profile, inside R650k. If that's more space than you need, the Kia Sorento is a smaller, cheaper premium 7-seat; for a new car with a warranty, 7 airbags and a Global NCAP 5★-adult result, the Mahindra XUV700 AX7L.

Honest caveats

  1. Hijacking scores are a proxy, and the weakest data in this report. SA publishes most-hijacked model lists (Hilux, Polo, Ranger, Fortuner, NP200, D-Max lead) but no per-model rate. Each car here is graded in one of three tiers, stated on its card: listed — named in a published list, graded by position; donor — unlisted, but shares parts with a listed high-volume model, so graded up from it; unlisted — no list presence and no listed donor, held in a neutral 5.5–6.0 band. The lists are raw counts, so they track sales volume and structurally under-represent low-volume models: an unlisted grade is absence of evidence, not evidence of absence. That is exactly why the unlisted tier is neutral and never penalised.
  2. These grades were audited and corrected in July 2026. An earlier version graded on body construction — every ladder-frame bakkie-derived SUV was rated high-risk — which penalised the Ford Everest, Isuzu MU-X and Mitsubishi Pajero Sport, none of which appear in any of the four published lists, while under-rating the Suzuki Ertiga and Chery Tiggo 8, which do. The construction heuristic is gone; grading now follows the mechanism the sources actually describe — parts demand and volume. On my default weighting the leading ten were unaffected and the cars just behind them moved; the grades themselves are what changed, and they are listed on every card.
  3. The extra ≤4 penalty is a deliberate choice, not a measured figure. It applies a flat −0.5 to any model rated ≤4/10. Post-audit it reaches only cars actually named in a published list — of the 36 scored here, the Toyota Fortuner and the Suzuki Ertiga. Inferred grades sit above the threshold by design: an inference isn't strong enough evidence to carry a penalty. It is a dial, and now it's your dial — set it to zero in the panel above and watch where the Fortuner lands. You no longer have to take my word for any of this.
  4. Any car's risk can be cut sharply. A tracker means recovery is often within hours; gear-locks, secure parking and awareness of follow-home hotspots materially reduce exposure. The scores rank the target profile, not your driveway.
  5. Prices are representative asking figures (AutoTrader / Cars.co.za, July 2026), not quotes — price the actual cars you find, and buy the youngest / lowest-km example (several used leaders' factory warranties lapse ~2026).
  6. Crash protocols differ (Euro / Global / ANCAP / ASEAN, tested in different years and markets) — all genuine independent results; none guessed. Where a car's SA spec may differ from the tested car, the write-up says so.
  7. Get any used car independently inspected (AA / dealer) with a full service-history and finance/accident check before buying.
